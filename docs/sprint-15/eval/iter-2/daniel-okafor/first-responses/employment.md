## What's Actually Load-Bearing

### 1. The statutory framework for varying employment contracts

Under UK common law, an employment contract can only be lawfully varied if:

**(a) There is mutual agreement** — both employer and employee consent to the change, OR

**(b) There is a valid flexibility/variation clause** — the contract contains clear wording permitting the employer to make the specific type of change (in this case, varying working location or hours) without the employee's individual consent.

If neither applies and the employer imposes the change, the employee may have grounds for **constructive dismissal** (section 95 ERA 1996) or **breach of contract**.

### 2. For your hybrid policy specifically

Working location (office vs. home) is a **fundamental term of the employment contract**. Changing "3 days office, 2 flexible" involves:

- **Location of work** — this is implicit in the contract for most employees; if the written statement says "based at [address]", changing this is a variation
- **Hours/times** — if "flexible" means varying which days, that's also a term change

**The load-bearing question:** Does each employee's contract contain a flexibility clause broad enough to permit the employer to impose fixed office days without individual agreement? If yes, is the clause clear and specific enough?

Acas guidance is clear: a flexibility clause "must be clear that your employer can make the type of change they want to make."

### 3. The "fire and rehire" risk

If there's no flexibility clause (or it's not clear enough), and employees don't consent, the employer can technically dismiss and rehire on new terms — but:

- The **Statutory Code of Practice on Dismissal and Re-engagement** (in force since July 2024) applies
- Failure to follow it can result in uplift of up to 25% on any tribunal compensation
- This is now heavily scrutinised, particularly for blanket policy changes affecting multiple employees

### 4. What to do

| If... | Then... |
|-------|---------|
| Contracts have clear flexibility clause covering location | Give written notice of the change (one month is best practice per NI direct.gov.uk guidance) |
| No flexibility clause | **Get written consent** from each employee, OR negotiate new contracts |
| Employees object | Consult, consider alternatives, and if still required, follow fair procedure including potential redundancy consultation if workforce-wide |

## Summary

**Section 12 ERA 1996 is a red herring here** — it's about pay statement requirements. The real statutory load-bearing for your hybrid policy is:

1. **Contract flexibility clause** — does it exist and cover this change?
2. **Written notice** — give written notice of the change (best practice: one month, as per standard wrongful dismissal timeline guidance)
3. **Mutual agreement or valid clause** — one of these is required; otherwise it's breach/constructive dismissal risk
4. **Fair procedure** — if forcing change, follow the statutory code on fire-and-rehire
