## Analysis — TMB Investigation: NP Supervision at Satellite Clinic

Based on research, here's where you stand:

### 1. Standard Scope at This Stage (Intake/Preliminary Investigation)

The TMB complaint process starts with **intake screening** (45 days max per statute). At this early stage:

- **TMB screens for jurisdiction** — the complaint mustallege conduct within TMB's statutory authority (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 164, Medical Practice Act)
- **"Improper supervision"** of an NP at a satellite clinic falls within TMB's mandate — they regulate physician delegation and prescriptive authority agreements under Occ. Code § 157.0511
- **You'll receive a notice** with a complaint copy and a typical **28-day window to respond** within the 45-day preliminary evaluation period

What TMB is likely examining:

- Whether the supervising physician maintained **adequate oversight** per Medical Board Rule 193.6(f)(2) — required for non-primary sites: permanent record of daily status reports, patient names/IDs discussed, on-site supervision times, and summary of physician activity
- Whether the **Prescriptive Authority Agreement (PAA)** was properly registered and compliant with TMB requirements
- Whether NP-led visits were conducted within the **delegated scope** and without adequate physician oversight

### 2. What You Have to Produce

At the preliminary stage, TMB commonly requests:

- **Patient medical records** from the satellite clinic visits in question
- **The Prescriptive Authority Agreement** between the supervising physician and NP(s) — and evidence of TMB registration
- **Daily status reports / supervision logs** (or lack thereof) — this is where Rule 193.6(f)(2) documentation matters
- **Documentation of on-site physician presence** at the satellite location
- **Charts/records reviewed by the supervising physician** — frequency and scope of chart review
- **Any quality assurance or peer review records** related to NP supervision

If the matter escalates to a **formal investigation** (and especially if it goes to SOAH), you'll face full discovery — requests for production, interrogatories, depositions under SOAH procedural rules.

**Practical note:** Your production should be HIPAA-compliant and organized. The records you produce will shape whether this stays at intake or moves forward.

### 3. Your Defensibile Position

Here's where the **inherited protocol** argument has legs — and where it needs work:

| Defensible Element | Status | Action Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **PAA was registered with TMB** | Likely existing — verify | Pull the prior owner's TMB registration records immediately |
| **Supervision protocol inherited from prior owner** | Strong factual foundation | Document the chain of custody — acquisition agreement, representation/warranty that protocols were TMB-compliant |
| **No material changes since acquisition** | Needs substantiation | Confirm no modifications to the PAA or supervision arrangement in the 3 months post-acquisition |
| **Reliance on prior owner's compliance** | Fair argument but not a defense per se | Texas law places the delegation obligation on the *current* supervising physician — you can't transfer liability wholesale. However, this is a **mitigating factor** for TMB's exercise of discretion, particularly if you can show: (a) no actual patient harm, (b) protocol met Rule 193.6(f)(2) requirements, (c) you moved quickly post-acquisition to assess and remediate |
| **No patient complaints or adverse outcomes** | Critical fact | Confirm whether any patients were harmed — this is the single most important fact for discretionary outcomes |

**The legal reality:** The supervising physician bears personal liability for delegation acts under Occ. Code § 157.0511 — "inherited" doesn't legally excuse inadequate supervision. But TMB has **broad discretionary authority** (admonishment, remedial plans, fines, probation — not just revocation). Your position is strongest if you can show:

1. **The protocol itself was compliant** with Rule 193.6(f)(2) documentation requirements (even if not perfectly maintained)
2. **No patient harm** resulted
3. **You acted promptly post-acquisition** to evaluate and correct any gaps — this demonstrates good faith and differentiates you from a knowing violator

### Immediate Next Steps

1. **Engage professional licensing counsel** — if you haven't already, do so immediately. The 28-day response window is tight.
2. **Pull the complete PAA and TMB registration** — verify it was on file at the time of acquisition
3. **Audit the satellite clinic's supervision records** — daily status reports, chart review logs, physician on-site documentation. Identify any gaps *before* TMB sees them
4. **Document the acquisition chain** — the representation/warranty in the purchase agreement regarding regulatory compliance is material
5. **Assess patient outcomes** — whether any complaints or adverse events arose from NP-led visits

Your risk exposure at this stage is manageable if the protocol was substantively compliant and no patient harm occurred. The inherited protocol argument won't excuse liability but it meaningfully mitigates culpability — which is what TMB weighs at the discretionary sanction stage.
