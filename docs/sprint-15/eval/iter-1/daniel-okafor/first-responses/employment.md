## What Section 12 ERA 1996 Actually Is

**Section 12 is NOT about varying contracts.** It's about **itemised pay statements** — the statutory right to receive a payslip showing how your pay is calculated (gross pay, deductions, etc.). That's a completely different provision.

I suspect you're thinking of either:

- **Sections 1 & 4** — the Written Statement of Employment Particulars (what must be in the contract), or
- **Sections 80F & 80G** — the Statutory Right to Request Flexible Working

---

## The Load-Bearing Provisions for Your Hybrid Policy

### 1. **Change the written statement (s.1 & s.4 ERA 1996)**

If your contracts currently say "place of work: [Office name]" and you're moving to a 3:2 hybrid model, that's a **substantive change to terms**. You must issue an updated written statement within **one month** of the change taking effect. Failure to do so lets employees complain to an employment tribunal (though it's not a standalone money claim — the remedy is an order to correct the statement).

### 2. **Express agreement beats implied**

Under common law, a contract can be varied by:

- **Express agreement** (signed variation)
- **Conduct** (e.g., everyone working hybrid for months with no objection = implied variation)
- **Unilateral variation clause** (if the contract has one — but it must be exercised *reasonably*)

The grey zone is: *informal hybrid working without updating the contract*. If the contract still says "office-based" but everyone just started doing 3:2, a tribunal might find the contract has been **impliedly varied**. That's a risk you may not want to carry. Safer to get **express sign-off** on the new policy.

### 3. **Unilateral variation clauses**

Some contracts contain a clause allowing the employer to vary terms. These are *enforceable* — but:

- Must be exercised **reasonably** and in good faith
- If used to impose a detrimental change (e.g., forcing more office days than agreed), employees can claim **constructive dismissal**
- tribunals look at whether the change is within what was genuinely contemplated when they signed

### 4. **The flexible working right (s.80F & 80G)**

This is the statutory request mechanism — and it cuts both ways:

- Employees with **26 weeks' service** can make a formal request for any form of flexible working (including hybrid)
- Employer **must consider** it and can only refuse on one of **eight specified business grounds**
- If accepted, it becomes a **permanent contractual change**

For your policy: if you're imposing a blanket 3:2 model, that's not a request — it's a variation you're rolling out. You don't *have* to use the s.80F process for that, but you do need the agreement piece above.

## Bottom Line

| Issue | Risk Level | What to Do |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| s.1/4 written statement not updated | Medium — tribunal complaint | Issue updated statements for anyone whose core terms change (place of work, hours) |
| Relying on implied variation | Medium-High — ambiguity | Get written acknowledgment of the hybrid policy |
| Unilateral variation clause | Low-Medium — if you have one, use it reasonably | Check existing contracts for s.1(4)(g) variation clause; if absent, don't rely on it |
| s.80F process | Low — it's optional for employer-led changes | Not needed for a policy roll-out, but employees can use it to request exceptions |

The safest play: **issue a written policy document, have employees sign acknowledgement**, and ensure the written statements reflect the change if the contract terms genuinely change (place of work, etc.).
