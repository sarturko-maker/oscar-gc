RFQ Review Playbook — In-House Commercial Counsel

This playbook covers any inbound Request for Quotation (RFQ) or Request for Proposal (RFP) issued by a prospective customer or procurement-led buyer where our business is the bidder. It applies regardless of vertical, deal size, or counterparty. Treat it as the senior reviewer in the room: it tells you what to read first, what to look for, and what to push back on.

# 1. Triage order — read in this sequence

Reviewing every page of every document in linear filename order is the wrong approach. Read in importance order:

1. **Cover letter / RFQ invitation.** Establishes deadlines, scoring weights, the buyer's stated procurement objectives, the contact, and the disqualification triggers. Always read this first. Note disqualification rules — they reshape the scope of negotiable items.
2. **Master Supply Agreement / Master Services Agreement.** The load-bearing legal document. Most of the negotiation effort lives here. Identify asymmetries (see §2).
3. **Buyer's General Terms and Conditions (T&Cs / GTCs).** Often the trap layer. Routinely conflicts with the MSA. Check the order of precedence (see §3). Treat T&Cs as the buyer's true position — the MSA may have been edited for the bid but the GTCs are the standing house terms.
4. **Service Level Agreement.** Targets, penalties, escalation matrix, material-breach triggers. Penalty arithmetic compounds — read with a calculator.
5. **Compliance / ESG annex.** Modern slavery, anti-bribery, sanctions, environmental, data protection. Disqualification risk here is usually higher than commercial.
6. **Pricing schedule.** Read last from a legal perspective — the commercial team owns the numbers, but flag clauses that bind pricing behaviour (most-favoured-customer, open-book costing, audit of pricing).
7. **Questionnaire / RFP response template.** Skim only — flag any question that asks the bidder to commit to terms outside the MSA.

# 2. The asymmetries to look for in the MSA

Read every operative clause looking specifically for these recurring patterns. Each is a routine push-back point.

## 2.1 Indemnities

- **One-sided indemnity** running only from supplier to buyer (or only buyer to supplier in a buyer-side deal) — push for mutual where appropriate.
- **Uncapped indemnity** sitting outside the cap-of-liability clause — the most expensive class of risk; almost always negotiable to a separate higher cap.
- **Indemnity scope drift** — covering breach of contract, negligence, defect, IP, and "any applicable law" in one sweep. Decompose into named heads of liability.

## 2.2 Liability caps and exclusions

- **Asymmetric caps** — supplier capped at 12 months' fees while buyer is capped at a fixed (often tiny) amount, or uncapped. The MSA should have symmetric default caps with named carve-outs.
- **Carve-outs reading more like exclusions** — IP infringement, confidentiality, data protection, fraud, death/personal injury. Confirm the carve-outs are mutual.
- **Pure economic loss / loss of profit exclusions** — fine in principle, but check the carve-outs from the exclusion don't reintroduce uncapped exposure via the back door.

## 2.3 Termination

- **Termination for convenience by buyer only** — without a corresponding right for the supplier, this turns a 3-year framework into a 30-day rolling arrangement. Either remove or reciprocate; if neither, secure protective compensation (wind-down costs, work-in-progress, stranded costs, demobilisation).
- **Termination triggers that include "change of control to a competitor"** — push to define "competitor" tightly. Without definition this is buyer discretion.
- **Termination for insolvency / change of control / material breach** — typically reciprocal in principle but check the cure periods are workable (30 days is standard).

## 2.4 IP

- **Foreground IP assignment to buyer** for any work product — heavily restrict scope. The bidder's tooling, methodologies, frameworks, and pre-existing know-how (Background IP) must be carved out. Joint Foreground IP arising from the work should usually be jointly owned or licensed back to the supplier, not assigned absolutely to the buyer.
- **Perpetual royalty-free licence to background IP** "embedded in" deliverables — usually too broad. Limit to the scope necessary to use the deliverables for their intended purpose.

## 2.5 Audit rights

- Check the audit clause for scope (records only, or premises + systems + sub-contractors), notice period (5 BD is workable; 24h is not), frequency (twice a year is workable; "at any time" is not), cost (party-bearing rules), and survival period (4-6 years is reasonable; longer is not).

## 2.6 Payment

- **Payment terms longer than 60 days** are aggressive in most UK / EU markets. 90 days should be challenged unless reflected in pricing.
- **Late-payment interest at the statutory minimum** — under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998 the statutory rate is 8% over the Bank of England base rate. Confirm this is what is intended; aggressive buyers sometimes drop the rate.
- **Set-off rights** unilateral to the buyer — push for mutual or remove.

## 2.7 Pricing protections

- **Most-favoured-customer (MFC) clauses** — combined with audit rights they create a continuous compliance burden. Either resist or limit to genuinely comparable products on genuinely comparable terms within a defined customer cohort.
- **Open-book costing** for nominated SKUs — confirm what "open-book" means in practice, who sees the data, and the consequences of margin compression.
- **Unilateral price-adjustment rights by buyer** — should not exist. Pricing changes require mutual agreement.

## 2.8 Governing law and jurisdiction

- Check governing law matches the bidder's preferred forum. Local-buyer-jurisdiction is standard but problematic for cross-border bids.
- Check the dispute-resolution path — courts vs. arbitration. Arbitration is usually preferable for cross-border deals; courts for domestic.

# 3. Battle of forms

When buyers issue an RFQ pack containing both an MSA and standalone T&Cs:

- The **T&Cs almost always state they prevail over any other document.** That clause is often in the GTCs themselves and is the first place to look.
- The MSA may contain a contradictory order-of-precedence clause (MSA prevails). Flag both clauses for the negotiation table.
- Pricing schedules, SLAs, and annexes often carry their own clauses purporting to override the MSA. Map the precedence stack on a single page before negotiating.
- The bidder's accepted position should normally be: **bid response wording > redlined MSA > buyer's GTCs**, with any annexes ranking below the MSA. If the buyer refuses to flip the precedence, the bidder is effectively bidding on the T&Cs alone — price accordingly.

# 4. Procurement-specific risk areas

These show up in procurement-led bids more than in standalone deals:

- **Audit rights extending to subsidiaries and sub-suppliers** — supplier may not be able to grant these. Limit to the supplier and named principal sub-suppliers.
- **ESG attestation requirements** that demand a certifying signature at director level — confirm the bidder can attest accurately, particularly around Scope 3 emissions and SBTi targets. Negative attestations on disclosed non-compliance are usually safer than over-claiming.
- **Data Processing Annex by reference** — never accept "as updated by the controller from time to time" wording. The DPA must be fixed at a known version with negotiated change procedure.
- **Cyber-security minima** baked into the compliance annex (Cyber Essentials Plus, MFA, encryption, pen-testing, incident-notification windows) — these are often achievable but the timelines and audit rights need scoping.
- **Strategic stock holding** — confirm the supplier is being compensated for stock-holding cost, including capital, insurance, and obsolescence risk.
- **Change-of-control notification clauses** broader than the corresponding termination right — these can interfere with M&A processes. Limit to the termination trigger.

# 5. Output format

After review, produce two deliverables:

1. **Redlined draft of the load-bearing document** (typically the MSA — sometimes the buyer's GTCs if those are the prevailing instrument). Use track changes; include both substantive edits and short comments explaining the rationale for each material change.
2. **Cover memo to the business** — one page, three sections:
   - **Top-3 walk-away issues.** The matters on which the bidder should be willing to refuse the contract if not resolved. Three is the practical limit — beyond three the business will pick favourites and you lose the others.
   - **Top-5 high-priority push-back items.** Significant economic or risk exposures that should be pushed but are not deal-breakers.
   - **Acceptable / nice-to-have items.** Everything else, with a one-line note per item.

The memo is what the business reads; the redline is what gets traded. Pitch the memo to a non-lawyer audience.

# 6. Sign-off and escalation

- Walk-away issues must be escalated to the General Counsel or designated approver before being conceded.
- Compliance non-conformance (Modern Slavery, Bribery Act, sanctions, data protection) must be escalated to the appropriate function (compliance, DPO) before being conceded.
- Pricing concessions beyond agreed envelopes must be escalated to the commercial sponsor.

# 7. What this playbook does NOT cover

- Specific pricing benchmarks (commercial team's domain).
- Jurisdiction-specific tax structuring (specialist tax counsel).
- Industry-specific regulatory obligations (e.g. medical-device QMS, financial-services outsourcing, defence procurement) — bring in subject-matter specialists.
- Disputes-handling once a contract is live (handled under the dispute-resolution clause of the executed contract).

This playbook supports first-pass review by a competent commercial counsel. Anything outside its scope, escalate.
