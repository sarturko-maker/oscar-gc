NDA Review Playbook — In-House Commercial Counsel

This playbook governs first-pass review of any inbound Non-Disclosure / Confidentiality Agreement (NDA) where our business is being asked to receive Confidential Information, disclose Confidential Information, or both. It applies regardless of counterparty type, deal context, or originating jurisdiction.

# 1. Triage rubric — GREEN / YELLOW / RED

Every NDA gets a single-letter verdict. Reviewers should be able to triage a typical 2-3 page NDA in 5-10 minutes using this rubric.

- **GREEN** — balanced mutual or bilateral NDA with standard carve-outs (publicly known, prior knowledge, third-party receipt, independent development, compelled disclosure), reasonable term (1-3 years operative + 1-5 years survival), our preferred or a neutral governing law, no unusual restrictions. Can go straight to signature without legal review.
- **YELLOW** — material but negotiable issue: long survival (more than 7 years), broad CI definition (catches publicly-available information, or any information disclosed in any context), one-sided obligations, non-solicitation clauses, no-publicity clauses, unfavourable governing law but otherwise normal. Needs legal review and one round of redlining; usually closeable.
- **RED** — at least one of the following: perpetual / indefinite term, no carve-outs at all, extraterritorial jurisdiction we don't operate in (and which would expose us to costly forum-shopping), liquidated-damages or per-incident penalty clauses, non-compete clauses dressed up as NDA terms, IP assignment language smuggled into the confidentiality terms, no-residuals carve-out. Needs proper legal involvement; not signed without partner-level decision.

# 2. The flag list — what to look for

Read every NDA looking for these. Each is a routine triage trigger.

## 2.1 Term and survival

- **Indefinite / perpetual obligations** → RED. No business case justifies giving an unlimited-duration confidentiality undertaking; it surfaces 20 years later when no one remembers why and creates discovery exposure.
- **Survival of more than 7 years** → YELLOW. Push to 3 years from disclosure unless there's a good reason (e.g., long-running regulatory matter, trade-secret content).
- **Operative term of more than 5 years** → YELLOW. Most discussions are over within 12-18 months.

## 2.2 Definition of Confidential Information

- **Captures publicly available information** → YELLOW or RED depending on phrasing. "Information that is publicly available but compiled or contextualised by the disclosing party" is YELLOW; "any information disclosed in any context" is RED.
- **Excludes residuals carve-out** → YELLOW. Sophisticated NDAs allow residuals retained in unaided memory of personnel; absence is a flag.
- **Captures derivative works** → YELLOW. Standard wording covers the information itself, not derivative analysis the receiving party generates from public sources informed by the discussions.

## 2.3 Carve-outs

- **Missing standard carve-outs** (publicly known, prior knowledge, third-party receipt, independent development, compelled disclosure) → YELLOW or RED.
- **Single-carve-out clauses** ("only information already in our written records prior to disclosure") → RED.
- **Compelled-disclosure carve-out without prompt-notice obligation** → YELLOW. Buyer wants notice so they can seek a protective order; absence is a flag.

## 2.4 Governing law and jurisdiction

- **Governing law from a jurisdiction the receiving party doesn't operate in** → YELLOW. Push for our local law or a neutral choice (English law is widely acceptable).
- **Exclusive jurisdiction in counterparty's home court** → YELLOW. Cost-of-litigation flag.
- **Extraterritorial reach** ("subject to the laws of [Texas / California / Singapore] without regard to conflict-of-laws principles") → YELLOW or RED. Conflict-of-laws principles waiver is sometimes a smuggled forum-shopping play.

## 2.5 Beyond-confidentiality clauses

These are often the real risk — clauses that aren't about confidentiality at all but get smuggled in under an NDA cover.

- **Non-compete** → RED. Confidentiality and non-compete are different agreements; combining them is usually a sign of buyer trying to lock the seller out of a market via an NDA negotiating window.
- **Non-solicitation** → YELLOW if narrow (employees actually in contact, 12 months); RED if broad (any employee, any indirect approach, 24+ months).
- **IP assignment** → RED. NDAs are not the place for IP transfer. If the counterparty wants assignment, that's a separate paid licence or development agreement.
- **Liquidated damages** → RED. The party drafting an NDA with a $500k-per-incident clause has thought hard about how to extract value from an alleged breach; we shouldn't sign without serious legal involvement.
- **No-publicity clauses extending beyond announcing the deal** → YELLOW. Standard "no public statements about the discussions" is fine; "no statements about the counterparty for 2 years post-termination" is YELLOW.
- **Reverse-engineering restrictions** in a confidentiality clause → YELLOW or RED depending on context. Make sense for software; not for general commercial NDAs.

## 2.6 Mutual vs bilateral

- **Mutual** NDAs apply confidentiality obligations to both parties. Preferred default for any two-way commercial discussion.
- **Bilateral / one-way** NDAs apply obligations only to the receiving party. Acceptable when we are clearly receiving Confidential Information for a defined evaluation purpose (e.g., evaluating a vendor's pricing methodology). Not acceptable when there is genuine two-way disclosure.
- **One-way running TO us as discloser** → GREEN typically.
- **One-way running FROM us as receiver** without a clear scope → YELLOW.

# 3. Output format

For batch review (multiple NDAs), produce a single table:

| # | Counterparty | Verdict | Top Issues | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [Counterparty A] | GREEN | none | Sign |
| 2 | [Counterparty B] | YELLOW | Long survival, no residuals | Push to 3+3, add residuals, then sign |
| 3 | [Counterparty C] | RED | Perpetual, no carve-outs, $500k LDs | Escalate to GC; not signable as drafted |

For per-NDA detail, follow with one paragraph each on the RED and YELLOW items, naming the clause and the proposed redline shape.

# 4. Sign-off rules

- **GREEN** — counsel sign or commercial sponsor sign with no legal involvement required.
- **YELLOW** — counsel reviews and signs after redline; flag survival/CI-definition changes to the relationship lead.
- **RED** — General Counsel must approve. Senior commercial sponsor must sign.

# 5. Counterparty correspondence

When responding to a RED NDA with substantive issues, write to the counterparty's legal team (not their commercial team — they will not have authority to amend their standard NDA). Use this shape:

1. Thanks for the NDA.
2. Identify the 2-3 issues we cannot accept (do not enumerate every YELLOW item — pick the ones that genuinely block).
3. For each, name the clause and propose specific alternative wording.
4. Offer to discuss; do not propose multiple alternatives in the first message (creates the impression of unlimited negotiating room).

# 6. When this playbook does not apply

- Settlement NDAs (different shape; usually negotiated with disputes counsel).
- M&A confidentiality agreements (typically NDA + standstill + exclusivity; specialist M&A counsel).
- Government / classified-information NDAs (specialist regulatory counsel).
- Employment-related confidentiality undertakings (HR counsel).

This playbook covers commercial-counterparty NDAs. Anything else, escalate.
